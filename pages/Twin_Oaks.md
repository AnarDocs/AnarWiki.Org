# Twin Oaks Community

**Twin Oaks Community** is a
<a href="secular" class="wikilink" title="secular">secular</a>
<a href="intentional_community" class="wikilink"
title="intentional community">intentional community</a> and
<a href="ecovillage" class="wikilink" title="ecovillage">ecovillage</a>
of approximately 100 people living on 450 acres in Louisa County,
Virginia. Founded in 1967, it represents one of the longest-running and
most successful examples of
<a href="anarchist" class="wikilink" title="anarchist">anarchist</a>
organization in North America. While Twin Oaks doesn't explicitly
identify as anarchist, its core values of
<a href="cooperation" class="wikilink"
title="cooperation">cooperation</a>,
<a href="egalitarianism" class="wikilink"
title="egalitarianism">egalitarianism</a>,
<a href="nonviolence" class="wikilink"
title="nonviolence">nonviolence</a>,
<a href="sustainability" class="wikilink"
title="sustainability">sustainability</a>, and income sharing, combined
with its rejection of hierarchical authority and embrace of
<a href="direct_democracy" class="wikilink"
title="direct democracy">direct democracy</a>, make it a significant
case study in applied anarchist principles.

## Overview

Twin Oaks was founded by a group of eight individuals inspired by B.F.
Skinner's behaviorist utopian novel *Walden Two*.[^1] However, the
community quickly evolved beyond Skinner's vision, abandoning
behaviorist principles in favor of genuinely egalitarian organization
that demonstrates many core anarchist values in practice.

As one Twin Oaks member noted in a 1990 speech on anarchism: "I want to
tell you about a place called Twin Oaks... they don't bill themselves as
anarchists, but rather they use words like egalitarian, feminist and
'embracing diversity' - it amounts to the same thing."[^2]

The community operates without traditional capitalist wage
relationships, police, or coercive hierarchy. Instead, it has developed
sophisticated systems of
<a href="mutual_aid" class="wikilink" title="mutual aid">mutual aid</a>,
<a href="consensus_decision-making" class="wikilink"
title="consensus decision-making">consensus decision-making</a>, and
<a href="restorative_justice" class="wikilink"
title="restorative justice">restorative justice</a> that demonstrate the
viability of anarchist organization on a significant scale.

## Culture

### Anti-Hierarchical Values

Twin Oaks culture explicitly rejects domination and hierarchy in favor
of cooperation and mutual support. As their core values statement
emphasizes, the community is built around
<a href="egalitarianism" class="wikilink"
title="egalitarianism">egalitarianism</a> and
<a href="nonviolence" class="wikilink"
title="nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, creating what one academic study
called "among the most non-sexist social systems in human history."[^3]

The community practices what they call "consent culture," assuming that
"personal boundaries will be respected and that all people will be
sensitive and tuned into interacting with and treating each other with
appropriate respect."[^4]

### Intersectional Politics

Twin Oaks strives for intersectional analysis, acknowledging "the
intersecting social structures of gender, race, social class, sexual
identity, religion, ability, and age" as interconnected systems of
oppression.[^5] The community has established teams like the Racial
Equity Team (RET) and Racial Equity & Advocacy Leaders (REAL) to
actively work on anti-oppression within the community.

Members engage in various forms of activism including indigenous rights,
women's marches, LGBTQ+ activism, Black Lives Matter actions, and Books
Behind Bars programs, though the community doesn't officially endorse
specific political actions.[^6]

### Conflict Resolution

Rather than relying on punishment or authority figures, Twin Oaks has
developed sophisticated conflict resolution systems based on
<a href="restorative_justice" class="wikilink"
title="restorative justice">restorative justice</a> principles. The
community uses mediation, facilitated meetings, and community support
through their Process Team rather than hierarchical enforcement.[^7]

As they note: "it isn't the existence of conflict that determines the
health of a group, but rather the manner in which a group does or
doesn't deal with conflict that determines its health."[^8]

## Organisation

### Labor Credit System

Twin Oaks' most revolutionary innovation is its
<a href="labor_credit_system" class="wikilink"
title="labor credit system">labor credit system</a>, which effectively
eliminates the wage-labor relationship central to capitalism. All
members work approximately 38 hours per week, with all labor valued
equally regardless of type - from income-generating work to childcare to
cleaning.[^9]

As scholar Allen Butcher explains, this system represents "the first
complete alternative economic system to that of monetary economics" and
"essentially the portal to a parallel reality existing within global
monetary economics."[^10]

The system operates on the principle that "reproductive work"
(traditionally "women's work") should be valued equally with all other
labor, making it fundamentally feminist in structure. Members earn
vacation time by working over their weekly quota, creating incentives
for contribution while maintaining equality.[^11]

### Modified Consensus Governance

While originally inspired by Skinner's "planner-manager" system, Twin
Oaks governance evolved into a form of participatory democracy that
resembles
<a href="sociocracy" class="wikilink" title="sociocracy">sociocracy</a>.
The community uses a modified consensus process where authority is
delegated to managers and planners, but these positions remain
accountable to the membership.[^12]

As one member describes it: "the Twin Oaks plannership is a self
perpetuating autocracy with a democratic cap" - planners choose their
successors, but 20% of the membership can veto any candidate.[^13]

Remarkably, when the community temporarily had no planners in recent
years, operations continued smoothly, demonstrating the decentralized
and resilient nature of their organizational structure. As the member
noted: "What has the effect been on the community? Almost nothing... The
internals of the community are both resilient and decentralized. We
don't need an executive for much of what we do."[^14]

### Direct Democracy Elements

Key decisions are made through community-wide processes rather than by
authorities. The community regularly holds meetings where all members
can participate in shaping policy and resolving conflicts. When the
normal governance structure fails, the community defaults to elections -
demonstrating their commitment to democratic participation over
hierarchical control.[^15]

## Economy

### Beyond Capitalism

Twin Oaks operates a
<a href="gift_economy" class="wikilink" title="gift economy">gift
economy</a> internally while interfacing with the capitalist market
externally. Members have no individual income and share all resources
collectively, with the community providing housing, food, healthcare,
and other necessities in exchange for labor contributions.[^16]

The community generates approximately \$600,000 annually through
collective businesses including tofu production, hammock manufacturing,
and book indexing.[^17] However, this external market engagement serves
the internal gift economy rather than individual accumulation.

### Collective Ownership

All major resources are held in common, including 18 vehicles, 7
residences, and extensive shared facilities for 100 people. Members
share a "clothing library" and have access to all community resources
based on need rather than ability to pay.[^18]

This collective ownership eliminates the competition and inequality
inherent in private property systems, demonstrating anarchist principles
of common ownership in practice.

### Sustainable Production

Twin Oaks produces a significant portion of its own food through organic
gardening and local agriculture. Members consume 70% less gasoline, 80%
less electricity, and 76% less natural gas per capita than their
neighbors, demonstrating how cooperative economics can reduce
environmental impact.[^19]

## Why Twin Oaks Represents Applied Anarchism

While Twin Oaks doesn't explicitly identify as anarchist, its practices
embody core anarchist principles:

### Rejection of Hierarchy

The community explicitly rejects hierarchical relationships in favor of
egalitarian cooperation. Authority positions are temporary, accountable,
and can be overruled by the membership. The labor credit system
eliminates the hierarchical employer-employee relationship.

### Direct Action and Mutual Aid

Rather than relying on external authorities, Twin Oaks members take
direct responsibility for meeting each other's needs. The community
provides healthcare, childcare, education, and conflict resolution
through collective effort rather than state or market mechanisms.

### Prefigurative Politics

Twin Oaks demonstrates <a href="prefigurative_politics" class="wikilink"
title="prefigurative politics">prefigurative politics</a> by creating
the kind of society anarchists envision rather than waiting for
revolutionary change. As one member explained in their 1990 speech:
"They decided to form a network of lovers, if you will, mostly quite
small, but the hierarchy lost its control over this issue."[^20]

### Anti-Authoritarianism

The community's resistance to television (to avoid importing "mainstream
values such as consumerism, violence, pre-packaged 'canned'
entertainment") and their emphasis on consent culture demonstrate active
resistance to authoritarian influences.[^21]

### Gender Liberation

Twin Oaks practices what they call "intersectional feminism" with much
less gender-based division of labor. "People of all different genders in
the community prepare food, fix cars, do child-care, use power tools,
etc."[^22] The equal valuation of reproductive labor represents a
fundamental challenge to patriarchal economic structures.

## Challenges and Limitations

### Scale and Replication

Twin Oaks has remained at approximately 100 members for decades,
suggesting potential limits to the scalability of this organizational
model. The community has instead grown through creating satellite
communities rather than expanding internally.[^23]

### Market Dependence

While internally operating as a gift economy, Twin Oaks remains
dependent on capitalist markets for survival. This creates ongoing
tensions between internal values and external necessities.

### Membership Turnover

The community experiences significant annual turnover (around 20%), and
some members report feeling "trapped" due to the difficulty of building
savings or equity for life outside the community.[^24]

### Childcare Struggles

Like many anarchist experiments, Twin Oaks has struggled with collective
childcare, eventually moving from "communal" to "collective" childcare
where parents retain primary responsibility for their children.[^25]

## Legacy and Significance

### Proving Anarchist Viability

Twin Oaks' 57-year existence demonstrates that anarchist principles can
work on a significant scale over extended periods. As Allen Butcher
notes, "No other secular communal society in America has existed as
long."[^26]

The community has successfully eliminated crime through community
self-organization rather than police: "Probably \$10 million in physical
plant, equipment, and tools and no locks in the whole place. Fourteen
cars and trucks with the keys in them and only one has been stolen in
the last 20 years."[^27]

### Network Building

Twin Oaks has helped establish several sister communities including
<a href="Acorn_Community" class="wikilink" title="Acorn Community">Acorn
Community</a>, <a href="Living_Energy_Farm" class="wikilink"
title="Living Energy Farm">Living Energy Farm</a>, and
<a href="East_Wind_Community" class="wikilink"
title="East Wind Community">East Wind Community</a>, creating a network
of anarchist-influenced intentional communities that Butcher describes
as potentially comprising "the basis for a comparative study of the
successes and failures of these related communities."[^28]

### Economic Innovation

The labor credit system represents a major contribution to anarchist
economic theory and practice, demonstrating that complex societies can
function without wage labor or monetary exchange for internal economic
relationships.

## Modern Relevance

Twin Oaks continues to serve as a "living laboratory" for anarchist
principles, hosting annual conferences and welcoming visitors who want
to experience alternatives to hierarchical organization. The community's
success challenges common assumptions about the necessity of state
authority and capitalist economics.

As one member concluded their 1990 speech: "So next time someone tells
you they are an anarchist, don't think about bombs, think about
freedom."[^29]

Twin Oaks proves that freedom is possible through cooperation, mutual
aid, and the rejection of hierarchical authority - core principles of
anarchist theory put into successful practice.
