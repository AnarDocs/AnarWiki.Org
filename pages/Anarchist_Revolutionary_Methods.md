# Methods For Achieving An Anarchist World

Anarchists have long proposed different methods of transitioning
societies toward non-hierarchical decentralised models, and some of
those (such in the case of
<a href="Rojava" class="wikilink" title="Rojava">Rojava</a> and
<a href="Revolutionary_Spain" class="wikilink"
title="Revolutionary Spain">Revolutionary Spain</a>) have had the
opportunity of being tried and to have had some success, despite violent
outside opposition.

The question of transition has been central to anarchist theory since
the movement's inception, with thinkers like
<a href="Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon" class="wikilink"
title="Pierre-Joseph Proudhon">Pierre-Joseph Proudhon</a>,
<a href="Mikhail_Bakunin" class="wikilink"
title="Mikhail Bakunin">Mikhail Bakunin</a>, and
<a href="Peter_Kropotkin" class="wikilink" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter
Kropotkin</a> offering different visions of how anarchist society might
emerge.

Despite their diversity, these various approaches share fundamental
commitments that distinguish anarchist strategy from authoritarian
socialist or reformist paths. All reject the seizure of state power as a
means to liberation, recognising that hierarchical means cannot produce
egalitarian ends. Instead, they emphasise prefiguration—embodying
anarchist values and social relations in the present rather than
deferring freedom to some post-revolutionary future. Whether through
gradual evolution or revolutionary rupture, these methods prioritise
direct action and self-organisation over electoral politics or vanguard
parties.

Most of these methods involve creating alternative institutions that
meet people's needs while demonstrating the viability of
non-hierarchical organisation. They share confidence in ordinary
people's capacity for self-governance and mutual aid, viewing hierarchy
not as necessary for social coordination but as an obstacle to human
flourishing. The multiplicity of approaches reflects anarchism's
anti-dogmatic character: rather than prescribing a single correct path,
anarchists recognise that different contexts, cultures, and moments may
call for different strategies, and that transformation will likely
involve combinations of multiple methods rather than adherence to any
single blueprint.

History shows that rapid, fundamental social change is
possible—sometimes occurring within years rather than generations, as
documented in <a href="Rapid_Social_Change" class="wikilink"
title="Rapid Social Change">Rapid Social Change</a>. Anarchists also
draw lessons from what to avoid: the catastrophic record of
<a href="Marxist_Leninist_States" class="wikilink"
title="Marxist Leninist States">Marxist Leninist States</a>, most of
which have collapsed or abandoned their stated principles, demonstrates
the bankruptcy of authoritarian roads to liberation. The deliberate
<a href="Marxist-Leninist_Persecution_Of_Anarchists" class="wikilink"
title="Marxist-Leninist Persecution Of Anarchists">Marxist-Leninist
Persecution Of Anarchists</a>—from the suppression of the
<a href="Kronstadt_rebellion" class="wikilink"
title="Kronstadt Uprising">Kronstadt Uprising</a> to the murder of
anarchists in Revolutionary Spain by Stalinist forces—stands as a
warning that authoritarians claiming to share liberatory goals have
historically been among anarchism's most dangerous enemies.

## Revolutionary Methods

### <a href=":Category:Insurrectionary_Anarchism" class="wikilink"
title="Insurrectionary Anarchism">Insurrectionary Anarchism</a>

**Insurrectionary Anarchism** = Small autonomous groups → Direct action
→ Disruption of state power → Inspirational acts → Mass uprising →
Autonomous zones

This approach envisions small autonomous groups engaging in direct
action and targeted attacks against symbols and infrastructure of state
and capitalist power, creating disruption that exposes the vulnerability
of hierarchical systems. These inspirational acts—ranging from sabotage
to expropriation to confrontational protest—are intended to spark wider
consciousness and emulation rather than function as a vanguard strategy.
As more people engage in autonomous resistance and the costs of
maintaining social control escalate, moments of crisis or state weakness
create openings for mass uprising. During these insurrectionary moments,
people establish autonomous zones where they organise their own affairs
without mediation by authority. The aim is not to seize state power but
to make existing power structures ungovernable while demonstrating the
viability of self-organisation.

Theorised by
<a href="Luigi_Galleani" class="wikilink" title="Luigi Galleani">Luigi
Galleani</a>, <a href="Alfredo_Bonanno" class="wikilink"
title="Alfredo Bonanno">Alfredo Bonanno</a>, and the contemporary
<a href="Invisible_Committee" class="wikilink"
title="Invisible Committee">Invisible Committee</a> in works like
*<a href="The_Coming_Insurrection" class="wikilink"
title="The Coming Insurrection">The Coming Insurrection</a>*. This
approach emphasises the "propaganda of the deed" concept developed by
<a href="Paul_Brousse" class="wikilink" title="Paul Brousse">Paul
Brousse</a> and practised by figures like
<a href="Ravachol" class="wikilink" title="Ravachol">Ravachol</a> and
<a href="Alexander_Berkman" class="wikilink"
title="Alexander Berkman">Alexander Berkman</a>.

Historical examples include the activities of the
<a href="Angry_Brigade" class="wikilink" title="Angry Brigade">Angry
Brigade</a> in 1970s Britain and more recently the establishment of
temporary autonomous zones during the
<a href="2020_George_Floyd_Uprising" class="wikilink"
title="2020 George Floyd Uprising">2020 George Floyd Uprising</a> and
the <a href="Capitol_Hill_Autonomous_Zone" class="wikilink"
title="Capitol Hill Autonomous Zone">Capitol Hill Autonomous Zone</a>
(CHAZ/CHOP) in Seattle.

### Gradual/Evolutionary Anarchism and Libertarian Education

**Gradual/Evolutionary Anarchism** = Education and propaganda → Cultural
change → Mutual aid networks → Dual power institutions → Gradual
absorption of state functions → Anarchist education as engine of
transformation

This approach envisions anarchist transformation beginning with
sustained efforts at education and propaganda that gradually shift
cultural values toward cooperation, mutual aid, and
anti-authoritarianism. Libertarian pedagogy is central to this process:
by creating schools and educational projects that embody anarchist
principles—student participation in decision-making, horizontal
relationships between teachers and learners, self-directed inquiry, and
integration of manual and intellectual work—educators demonstrate that
humans can develop and flourish without authoritarian structures.

As these values take root, people begin building mutual aid networks and
voluntary associations that meet their needs outside state structures.
Over time, these networks expand into dual power institutions—parallel
structures of healthcare, education, production, and distribution that
function alongside (and in competition with) state institutions. As
alternative structures prove more effective and humane, they steadily
absorb the functions previously monopolised by the state, which
gradually withers away as it becomes irrelevant to people's lives.

Students educated in libertarian environments carry forward
anti-authoritarian values as they mature, slowly transforming
consciousness across generations and eroding one of the key institutions
through which hierarchical social relations are reproduced. This process
might take generations, but proceeds without the violence and disruption
of revolutionary upheaval.

This approach was notably advocated by
<a href="Peter_Kropotkin" class="wikilink" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter
Kropotkin</a> in works like
*<a href="Mutual_Aid" class="wikilink" title="Mutual Aid">Mutual Aid</a>*
(1902), emphasising the evolutionary development of cooperative
institutions. <a href="Gustav_Landauer" class="wikilink"
title="Gustav Landauer">Gustav Landauer</a> similarly promoted gradual
cultural transformation, famously stating "The state is a social
relationship; a certain way of people relating to one another."[^1] The
pedagogical dimension was developed by
<a href="Francisco_Ferrer" class="wikilink"
title="Francisco Ferrer">Francisco Ferrer</a> through the
<a href="Modern_School" class="wikilink" title="Modern School">Modern
School</a> movement,
<a href="A.S._Neill" class="wikilink" title="A.S. Neill">A.S. Neill</a>'s
<a href="Summerhill_School" class="wikilink"
title="Summerhill School">Summerhill School</a>, and
<a href="Paulo_Freire" class="wikilink" title="Paulo Freire">Paulo
Freire</a>'s <a href="Critical_Pedagogy" class="wikilink"
title="Critical Pedagogy">Critical Pedagogy</a>.
<a href="Ivan_Illich" class="wikilink" title="Ivan Illich">Ivan
Illich</a>'s *<a href="Deschooling_Society" class="wikilink"
title="Deschooling Society">Deschooling Society</a>* provides further
theoretical foundations.

Real examples include the
<a href="Kibbutz" class="wikilink" title="Kibbutz">Kibbutz</a> movement
in historical Palestine, early
<a href="Intentional_Communities" class="wikilink"
title="Intentional Communities">Intentional Communities</a> like
<a href="Twin_Oaks" class="wikilink" title="Twin Oaks">Twin Oaks</a>,
the <a href="Transition_Towns" class="wikilink"
title="Transition Towns">Transition Towns</a> movement initiated by
<a href="Rob_Hopkins" class="wikilink" title="Rob Hopkins">Rob
Hopkins</a>, <a href="Democratic_Schools" class="wikilink"
title="Democratic Schools">Democratic Schools</a>, the
<a href="Unschooling" class="wikilink"
title="Unschooling">Unschooling</a> movement, and various
<a href="Free_Schools" class="wikilink" title="Free Schools">Free
Schools</a> and <a href="Liberation_Schools" class="wikilink"
title="Liberation Schools">Liberation Schools</a>.

## Combined Approaches

### <a href="Anarcho-Syndicalism" class="wikilink"
title="Syndicalism">Syndicalism</a>

**Syndicalism** = Strong unions → Industrial organisation → General
strike → Worker control of workplaces → Decommodification → Dismantling
of hierarchical structures

The syndicalist path begins with workers building militant, democratic
unions that organise entire industries rather than fragmentary crafts.
These unions develop increasing capacity for workers' self-management
through direct action, workplace struggles, and the creation of parallel
structures for production and distribution. As working-class
organisation strengthens across sectors, a general strike shuts down the
economy, denying the ruling class access to the surplus value extracted
from labour. During this moment of working-class power, workers take
direct control of their workplaces, expropriating the means of
production from capitalists and reorganising production according to
human need rather than profit. The economy is progressively
decommodified as workers coordinate production through voluntary
federations of workplace councils rather than markets or central
planning. State structures, having lost their economic foundation and
faced with self-organised workers who no longer need bosses or
politicians, are dismantled and replaced with voluntary confederation of
worker-controlled industries.

Developed by <a href="Fernand_Pelloutier" class="wikilink"
title="Fernand Pelloutier">Fernand Pelloutier</a>,
<a href="Rudolf_Rocker" class="wikilink" title="Rudolf Rocker">Rudolf
Rocker</a>, and the
<a href="Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" class="wikilink"
title="Industrial Workers of the World">Industrial Workers of the
World</a> (IWW) founders like
<a href="Big_Bill_Haywood" class="wikilink" title="Big Bill Haywood">Big
Bill Haywood</a>. <a href="Diego_Abad_de_Santillán" class="wikilink"
title="Diego Abad de Santillán">Diego Abad de Santillán</a> provided
detailed economic planning in
*<a href="After_the_Revolution" class="wikilink"
title="After the Revolution">After the Revolution</a>*.

Historical examples include
<a href="Revolutionary_Spain" class="wikilink"
title="Revolutionary Spain">Revolutionary Spain</a> (1936-1939) with the
<a href="CNT-FAI" class="wikilink" title="CNT-FAI">CNT-FAI</a>, the
<a href="IWW" class="wikilink" title="IWW">IWW</a>'s organising in early
20th century America, and contemporary examples like the
<a href="Solidarity_Federation" class="wikilink"
title="Solidarity Federation">Solidarity Federation</a> in the UK and
workplace occupations during Argentina's 2001 economic crisis.

### <a href="Libertarian_Municipalism" class="wikilink"
title="Municipalism">Municipalism</a>

**Municipalism** = <a href="Assemblies" class="wikilink"
title="Community assemblies">Community assemblies</a> → Confederated
municipalities → Dual power → Municipal ownership of economy →
Ecological integration → Decentralised confederation

This strategy begins by building community assemblies at the
neighbourhood level where residents make decisions collectively through
direct democracy. These assemblies gradually take on more local
functions, creating dual power against municipal governments while
building capacity for self-governance. Multiple municipalities form
confederations that coordinate across regions without surrendering local
autonomy, creating horizontal networks of mutual support. As these
confederated assemblies demonstrate their effectiveness, they
progressively take ownership of local economies, implementing
participatory planning and ensuring production serves community needs.
Ecological considerations are integrated throughout, with communities
coordinating to address environmental issues across bioregional scales.
Eventually, the confederation of municipalities replaces both capitalism
and the state with a directly democratic, ecologically integrated
society that operates through voluntary association rather than
hierarchy.

Developed primarily by <a href="Murray_Bookchin" class="wikilink"
title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a> in works like
*<a href="The_Ecology_of_Freedom" class="wikilink"
title="The Ecology of Freedom">The Ecology of Freedom</a>* and
*<a href="Post-Scarcity_Anarchism" class="wikilink"
title="Post-Scarcity Anarchism">Post-Scarcity Anarchism</a>*.
<a href="Janet_Biehl" class="wikilink" title="Janet Biehl">Janet
Biehl</a> has continued developing these ideas through the
<a href="Institute_for_Social_Ecology" class="wikilink"
title="Institute for Social Ecology">Institute for Social Ecology</a>.

Real examples include
<a href="Democratic_Confederalism" class="wikilink"
title="Democratic Confederalism">Democratic Confederalism</a> in
<a href="Rojava" class="wikilink" title="Rojava">Rojava</a>,
<a href="Zapatista" class="wikilink" title="Zapatista">Zapatista</a>
autonomous municipalities in
<a href="Chiapas" class="wikilink" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>,
participatory budgeting in cities like
<a href="Porto_Alegre" class="wikilink" title="Porto Alegre">Porto
Alegre</a>, and <a href="Barcelona_en_Comú" class="wikilink"
title="Barcelona en Comú">Barcelona en Comú</a>'s municipal platform.

### <a href="Platformism" class="wikilink"
title="Platformism">Platformism</a>

**Platformism** = Specific anarchist organisation → Theoretical and
tactical unity → Engagement in social movements → Revolutionary
situations → Coordinated action

The platformist approach advocates anarchists forming specific political
organisations unified around common theoretical analysis and tactical
orientation, rather than dispersing efforts across diffuse affinity
groups or broad coalitions. These organisations actively engage in
social movements—labour struggles, tenant organising, anti-fascist
action, environmental campaigns—bringing anarchist analysis and methods
while building meaningful relationships with working-class communities.
Through patient organising work and demonstrated commitment to social
struggles, anarchist organisations gain influence and help develop
revolutionary consciousness. When revolutionary situations emerge from
systemic crisis or heightened class conflict, the specific anarchist
organisation is positioned to coordinate effective collective action
across regions and sectors, providing strategic coherence without
reproducing vanguardist or authoritarian patterns.

Developed by
<a href="Nestor_Makhno" class="wikilink" title="Nestor Makhno">Nestor
Makhno</a>,
<a href="Peter_Arshinov" class="wikilink" title="Peter Arshinov">Peter
Arshinov</a>, and other exiled Russian anarchists in the
<a href="Delo_Truda" class="wikilink" title="Delo Truda">Delo Truda</a>
group who published *The Organizational Platform of the General Union of
Anarchists* (1926). Contemporary proponents include organisations like
the <a href="Anarchist_Federation" class="wikilink"
title="Anarchist Federation">Anarchist Federation</a> (UK),
<a href="Black_Rose_Anarchist_Federation" class="wikilink"
title="Black Rose Anarchist Federation">Black Rose Anarchist
Federation</a> (US), and the
<a href="Federação_Anarquista_do_Rio_de_Janeiro" class="wikilink"
title="Federação Anarquista do Rio de Janeiro">Federação Anarquista do
Rio de Janeiro</a> (Brazil).

## Technological Approaches

### <a href="Crypto-Anarchism" class="wikilink"
title="Crypto-Anarchism">Crypto-Anarchism</a>

**Crypto-Anarchism** = Using encryption and digital technologies to
create autonomous spaces

Crypto-anarchist strategy employs strong encryption, anonymising
networks, and decentralised digital infrastructure to create spaces
beyond state surveillance and control. By developing and deploying
technologies like encrypted communication, anonymous cryptocurrencies,
and peer-to-peer networks, individuals and communities can engage in
voluntary exchange and association without requiring state permission or
mediation. As these technologies become more accessible and widespread,
they progressively undermine state capacity for surveillance, taxation,
and regulation, while enabling economic and social organisation outside
formal legal structures. The proliferation of "cryptographic civil
disobedience" makes certain forms of state control technically
infeasible regardless of legal prohibitions, forcing recognition of
individual autonomy in digital spaces and potentially eroding state
power in physical spaces as digital and material worlds become
increasingly intertwined.

Pioneered by
<a href="Timothy_May" class="wikilink" title="Timothy May">Timothy
May</a> and the
<a href="Cypherpunk" class="wikilink" title="Cypherpunk">Cypherpunk</a>
movement. Contemporary examples include
<a href="Tor" class="wikilink" title="Tor">Tor</a>,
<a href="BitTorrent" class="wikilink" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a>,
various <a href="Darknet_Markets" class="wikilink"
title="Darknet Markets">Darknet Markets</a>,
<a href="Mesh_Networks" class="wikilink" title="Mesh Networks">Mesh
Networks</a>, and
<a href="Blockchain" class="wikilink" title="Blockchain">Blockchain</a>-based
<a href="Decentralized_Autonomous_Organizations" class="wikilink"
title="Decentralized Autonomous Organizations">Decentralized Autonomous
Organizations</a> (DAOs).

### Post-Scarcity Anarchism

**Post-Scarcity Anarchism** = Leveraging automation and technology to
enable anarchist social relations

This vision proposes leveraging automation, renewable energy, and
advanced production technologies to eliminate material scarcity, which
forms the basis for hierarchical control and resource hoarding. As
production becomes increasingly automated and decentralised through
technologies like advanced robotics, artificial intelligence, renewable
energy systems, and digital fabrication, human labour becomes less
necessary for meeting material needs. This technological abundance
undermines the economic foundations of both capitalism (which depends on
scarcity and wage labour) and state socialism (which depends on
centralised control of production). In conditions of genuine abundance,
hierarchical institutions lose their justification, and society can
reorganise around free access to goods and services, voluntary
association, and the pursuit of creative and social fulfilment rather
than survival. The transition involves both developing these
technologies through commons-based approaches and ensuring they serve
liberation rather than intensified control.

Theorised by <a href="Murray_Bookchin" class="wikilink"
title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a> in
*<a href="Post-Scarcity_Anarchism" class="wikilink"
title="Post-Scarcity Anarchism">Post-Scarcity Anarchism</a>* and further
developed by
<a href="Peter_Frase" class="wikilink" title="Peter Frase">Peter
Frase</a> in
*<a href="Four_Futures" class="wikilink" title="Four Futures">Four
Futures</a>*. <a href="Ursula_K._Le_Guin" class="wikilink"
title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula K. Le Guin</a>'s
*<a href="The_Dispossessed" class="wikilink" title="The Dispossessed">The
Dispossessed</a>* provides a fictional exploration of these concepts.

Examples include
<a href="Open_Source" class="wikilink" title="Open Source">Open
Source</a> software development,
<a href="3D_Printing" class="wikilink" title="3D Printing">3D
Printing</a> and
<a href="Fab_Labs" class="wikilink" title="Fab Labs">Fab Labs</a>,
<a href="Permaculture" class="wikilink"
title="Permaculture">Permaculture</a> design, and proposals for
<a href="Universal_Basic_Income" class="wikilink"
title="Universal Basic Income">Universal Basic Income</a> as a
transitional measure.

## Cultural Methods

### <a href="Situationism" class="wikilink"
title="Situationist Approaches">Situationist Approaches</a>

**Situationism** = Using art, culture, and spectacle to undermine
capitalist hegemony

The situationist strategy employs art, culture jamming, and subversive
interventions in everyday life to expose and undermine the "society of
the spectacle"—the system of mediated representations through which
capitalism maintains social control. Through techniques like
détournement (subversive reuse of capitalist cultural products), urban
drift (psychogeography that resists urban planning's control of
movement), and creation of temporary autonomous situations, activists
interrupt the passive consumption of commodified experience and reveal
possibilities for authentic, unmediated life. As these interventions
spread and people develop critical consciousness about their
recuperation into capitalist spectacle, they begin rejecting consumerist
identities and hierarchical social relations, instead creating
situations of genuine encounter, play, and collective creativity.

Developed by
<a href="Guy_Debord" class="wikilink" title="Guy Debord">Guy Debord</a>
and the <a href="Situationist_International" class="wikilink"
title="Situationist International">Situationist International</a>,
building on earlier work by the <a href="Surrealists" class="wikilink"
title="Surrealists">Surrealists</a> and
<a href="Dadaists" class="wikilink" title="Dadaists">Dadaists</a>. Key
concepts include <a href="détournement" class="wikilink"
title="détournement">détournement</a> and the critique of the
<a href="Society_of_the_Spectacle" class="wikilink"
title="Society of the Spectacle">Society of the Spectacle</a>.

Historical examples include the role of Situationists in
<a href="May_1968" class="wikilink" title="May 1968">May 1968</a> in
France,
<a href="Adbusters" class="wikilink" title="Adbusters">Adbusters</a>
magazine's culture jamming,
<a href="Critical_Mass" class="wikilink" title="Critical Mass">Critical
Mass</a> bicycle rides, and various forms of
<a href="Guerrilla_Theater" class="wikilink"
title="Guerrilla Theater">Guerrilla Theater</a> and
<a href="Street_Art" class="wikilink" title="Street Art">Street Art</a>.

## Post-Collapse and Disaster Approaches

These methods focus on preparing for, responding to, or taking advantage
of societal disruption and collapse.

### Anarchist Survivalism and Collapse Preparation

**Anarchist Survivalism / Collapse Anarchism** = Skill development →
Resource autonomy → Resilient communities → Mutual aid networks → Crisis
response → Seeds of post-collapse horizontal structures

This approach combines practical preparation with deliberate political
organisation. Practitioners develop skills in food production, water
management, renewable energy systems, natural building, wilderness
skills, and community organising, while creating networks of mutual
support and resource sharing. Rather than passively awaiting collapse or
attempting to prevent it, collapse anarchists actively prepare mutual
aid networks, skill-sharing systems, and horizontal organising capacity
specifically designed to activate during systemic breakdown—establishing
relationships of trust and reciprocity, developing crisis response
protocols, and creating "seeds" of post-collapse social organisation
such as knowledge bases, tool libraries, seed banks, and communication
networks.

As climate change, resource depletion, economic instability, and
political dysfunction intensify, these prepared communities become
increasingly self-reliant while also serving as nodes of mutual aid
during crises. When collapse occurs—whether gradual or sudden, partial
or total—these communities are positioned to not only survive but to
organise horizontal, cooperative responses that meet people's needs more
effectively than failing hierarchical systems. Their demonstrated
capacity for self-organisation during crisis provides both practical
alternatives to state and capitalist structures and lived proof that
people don't need rulers to coordinate collective life.

Influenced by <a href="James_Howard_Kunstler" class="wikilink"
title="James Howard Kunstler">James Howard Kunstler</a>'s
*<a href="The_Long_Emergency" class="wikilink"
title="The Long Emergency">The Long Emergency</a>*,
<a href="Dmitry_Orlov" class="wikilink" title="Dmitry Orlov">Dmitry
Orlov</a>'s collapse studies,
<a href="Rebecca_Solnit" class="wikilink" title="Rebecca Solnit">Rebecca
Solnit</a>'s *<a href="A_Paradise_Built_in_Hell" class="wikilink"
title="A Paradise Built in Hell">A Paradise Built in Hell</a>*
documenting mutual aid during disasters, and
<a href="James_C._Scott" class="wikilink" title="James C. Scott">James
C. Scott</a>'s *<a href="Against_the_Grain" class="wikilink"
title="Against the Grain">Against the Grain</a>*. Contemporary
collapse-aware anarchist writing is collected on platforms like
<a href="The_Anarchist_Library" class="wikilink"
title="The Anarchist Library">The Anarchist Library</a>.

Examples include <a href="Permaculture" class="wikilink"
title="Permaculture">Permaculture</a> communities,
<a href="Ecovillages" class="wikilink"
title="Ecovillages">Ecovillages</a>,
<a href="Food_Not_Bombs" class="wikilink" title="Food Not Bombs">Food
Not Bombs</a>, and disaster relief networks like
<a href="Common_Ground_Relief" class="wikilink"
title="Common Ground Relief">Common Ground Relief</a> after Hurricane
Katrina.

### Disaster Anarchism

**Disaster Anarchism** = Autonomous disaster response → Community
self-organisation → Demonstrating state inadequacy → Permanent
autonomous structures

This strategy involves organising autonomous disaster response networks
that can mobilise more quickly and effectively than state agencies
during hurricanes, floods, wildfires, pandemics, and other catastrophes.
When disasters strike, these networks provide immediate mutual
aid—distribution of supplies, search and rescue, medical care, community
kitchens, cleanup—while deliberately operating outside state control and
demonstrating community capacity for self-organisation. The contrast
between effective grassroots response and inadequate, bureaucratic, or
authoritarian state response delegitimises hierarchical institutions
while building practical experience and social bonds of cooperation.
Many people first encounter anarchist organising during these crisis
moments when abstract ideology matters less than concrete support.

As climate change and systemic instability make disasters increasingly
frequent, temporary autonomous structures can potentially evolve into
permanent alternative institutions, with mutual aid networks that
emerged for disaster response gradually taking on broader functions of
social coordination.

Documented by
<a href="Rebecca_Solnit" class="wikilink" title="Rebecca Solnit">Rebecca
Solnit</a> in *<a href="A_Paradise_Built_in_Hell" class="wikilink"
title="A Paradise Built in Hell">A Paradise Built in Hell</a>* and
practised by groups like
<a href="Occupy_Sandy" class="wikilink" title="Occupy Sandy">Occupy
Sandy</a>,
<a href="Food_Not_Bombs" class="wikilink" title="Food Not Bombs">Food
Not Bombs</a>, and various
<a href="Mutual_Aid_Networks" class="wikilink"
title="Mutual Aid Networks">Mutual Aid Networks</a> that expanded during
the <a href="COVID-19_Pandemic" class="wikilink"
title="COVID-19 Pandemic">COVID-19 Pandemic</a>.

### <a href="Solarpunk" class="wikilink"
title="Solarpunk Anarchism">Solarpunk Anarchism</a>

**Solarpunk Anarchism** = Sustainable technology → Climate adaptation →
Local resilience → Ecological reorganisation

This approach envisions building viable, attractive alternatives to both
capitalism and climate collapse through community-controlled renewable
energy systems, regenerative agriculture, appropriate technology, and
bioregional organisation. Rather than waiting for collapse or
revolution, solarpunk anarchists create prefigurative communities and
infrastructures that harmonise human flourishing with ecological
health—urban gardens, renewable energy cooperatives, open-source
technology networks, neighbourhood resilience projects. As climate
change intensifies and fossil fuel systems become economically and
politically untenable, these sustainable alternatives demonstrate
practical viability while embodying anti-authoritarian values.

The proliferation of locally and democratically controlled green
infrastructure gradually builds power to resist both corporate
greenwashing and authoritarian "green" developmentalism, while creating
material basis for decentralised, ecological society. This transition is
understood not as returning to primitivism but as moving toward
sophisticated, sustainable communities that have moved beyond both
hierarchy and ecocide.

Emerging from the
<a href="Solarpunk" class="wikilink" title="Solarpunk">Solarpunk</a>
literary and cultural movement, with influences from
<a href="Kim_Stanley_Robinson" class="wikilink"
title="Kim Stanley Robinson">Kim Stanley Robinson</a>'s climate fiction
and <a href="adrienne_maree_brown" class="wikilink"
title="adrienne maree brown">adrienne maree brown</a>'s
*<a href="Emergent_Strategy" class="wikilink"
title="Emergent Strategy">Emergent Strategy</a>*.

Examples include <a href="Transition_Towns" class="wikilink"
title="Transition Towns">Transition Towns</a>, urban
<a href="Community_Gardens" class="wikilink"
title="Community Gardens">Community Gardens</a>,
<a href="Renewable_Energy" class="wikilink"
title="Renewable Energy">Renewable Energy</a> cooperatives, and various
<a href="Climate_Justice" class="wikilink"
title="Climate Justice">Climate Justice</a> organisations employing
direct action tactics.

## Other Traditions

### <a href="Anarcho-Pacifism" class="wikilink"
title="Anarcho-Pacifism">Anarcho-Pacifism</a>

**Anarcho-Pacifism** = Prefigurative communities → Mass civil
disobedience → Non-cooperation with authority → Parallel institutions →
Gradual state withering

The anarcho-pacifist path begins with the establishment of prefigurative
communities that embody anarchist principles of non-violence, voluntary
association, and mutual aid, serving as living examples of alternative
social organisation. These communities engage in mass civil disobedience
campaigns and systematic non-cooperation with state authority, refusing
to participate in systems of domination while building parallel
institutions that meet community needs. As more people withdraw their
cooperation from hierarchical institutions and invest their energy in
autonomous alternatives, state power gradually erodes from lack of
compliance and legitimacy. The state eventually withers away not from
violent overthrow but from mass refusal to recognise its authority or
participate in its maintenance.

Developed by thinkers like
<a href="Leo_Tolstoy" class="wikilink" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo
Tolstoy</a>,
<a href="Mahatma_Gandhi" class="wikilink" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma
Gandhi</a> (though not strictly anarchist), and
<a href="Dorothy_Day" class="wikilink" title="Dorothy Day">Dorothy
Day</a> of the <a href="Catholic_Worker_Movement" class="wikilink"
title="Catholic Worker Movement">Catholic Worker Movement</a>.
<a href="A.J._Muste" class="wikilink" title="A.J. Muste">A.J. Muste</a>
and
<a href="Barbara_Deming" class="wikilink" title="Barbara Deming">Barbara
Deming</a> further refined anarchist approaches to nonviolent
resistance.

Historical examples include the
<a href="Catholic_Worker_Movement" class="wikilink"
title="Catholic Worker Movement">Catholic Worker Movement</a> houses of
hospitality,
<a href="Gandhi" class="wikilink" title="Gandhi">Gandhi</a>'s ashrams
and civil disobedience campaigns, and the
<a href="Plowshares_Movement" class="wikilink"
title="Plowshares Movement">Plowshares Movement</a>. Contemporary
examples include <a href="Extinction_Rebellion" class="wikilink"
title="Extinction Rebellion">Extinction Rebellion</a>'s civil
disobedience tactics and various
<a href="Occupy_Movement" class="wikilink"
title="Occupy Movement">Occupy Movement</a> encampments.

### <a href="Anarcho-Primitivism" class="wikilink"
title="Anarcho-Primitivism">Anarcho-Primitivism</a>

**Anarcho-Primitivism** = Revolutionary in its complete rejection of
industrial civilisation and call for its abandonment

Anarcho-primitivism calls for the complete abandonment of industrial
civilisation and a return to immediate, unmediated relationships with
the natural world and one another. This revolutionary rejection of
domestication, agriculture, and technology would involve humans
withdrawing from civilised society—whether through intentional community
formation or collapse-accelerating action—and redeveloping the skills
and social forms of hunter-gatherer existence. Proponents argue that
civilisation itself is inherently hierarchical and ecocidal, making
reform impossible; only its abandonment can create conditions for
genuinely free, egalitarian human life in balance with the biosphere.

Primarily developed by
<a href="John_Zerzan" class="wikilink" title="John Zerzan">John
Zerzan</a>,
<a href="Paul_Shepard" class="wikilink" title="Paul Shepard">Paul
Shepard</a>, and
<a href="Daniel_Quinn" class="wikilink" title="Daniel Quinn">Daniel
Quinn</a> (author of
*<a href="Ishmael" class="wikilink" title="Ishmael">Ishmael</a>*). This
approach draws on the work of
<a href="Marshall_Sahlins" class="wikilink"
title="Marshall Sahlins">Marshall Sahlins</a> and anthropological
studies of hunter-gatherer societies.

Real-world inspirations include indigenous resistance movements and
fictional depictions in works like
<a href="Edward_Abbey" class="wikilink" title="Edward Abbey">Edward
Abbey</a>'s *<a href="The_Monkey_Wrench_Gang" class="wikilink"
title="The Monkey Wrench Gang">The Monkey Wrench Gang</a>*.
